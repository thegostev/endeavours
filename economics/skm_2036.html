A speculative dispatch · December 2036
Tri-City rail · 2026 → 2036 · A speculative dispatch

Five lines, one bay. How Trójmiasto built a metro from a railway.

In December 2036, a Newag Impulse 3 will roll out of Wrzeszcz wearing the badge S5. It will mark the fifth line in a network that ten years earlier ran, in essence, as one. This is how that happened — year by year, contract by contract.

2026 · The starting line

Pomerania enters 2026 with the strongest commuter rail system in Poland and a network on the brink of becoming something else. SKM Trójmiasto carries 50.36 million passengers in 2025, an 11.47% national market share, and the region holds the country's record for rail intensity at 33.24 train trips per resident per year — roughly four times the EU average.

What is brittle is everything around the spine. Line 202 is half-modernized and half-construction site. Line 201 has been closed to passengers since June 2025 while Budimex rebuilds the alignment toward Kościerzyna. PKM Południe has a designer but no construction money. The Pakt Kolejowy of October 2024 is still a letter of intent.

The decade between 2026 and 2036 will turn out to be the most consequential in Tri-City rail since the original SKM was electrified in 1951. It is not the result of a single grand plan.

This narrative traces that decade year by year, ending with the five-line network and a ledger of what it cost. The speculation is anchored in contracts already signed by early 2026, demographic vectors visible in mobile-phone studies, and the funding instruments confirmed under FEnIKS 2021–2027, FEP Pomorze, KPK 2030, and the Pakt Kolejowy dla Pomorza.

§ 01 — The inheritanceWhat Tri-City has at the start of 2026

The starting position is paradoxically strong and brittle. SKM Trójmiasto's 50.36 million passengers in 2025 make it Poland's fourth-largest carrier. PKM, the regional government's audacious 880-million-złoty experiment opened in September 2015, has carried more than 36 million passengers in its first decade. Bilet Metropolitalny and the FALA payment system give the metropolis a single ticket across SKM, Polregio, ZTM Gdańsk, ZKM Gdynia and MZK Wejherowo.

What is brittle is everything around the spine. Line 202 Gdynia–Słupsk is half-modernized and half-construction site. The Lębork–Słupsk segment, originally promised for November 2026, has slipped to September 2027. The Gdynia Chylonia–Lębork contract — at 2.6 billion złoty net the most expensive single rail contract Pomorskie has ever managed — was signed only in December 2025; ZUE SA has 48 months to deliver four tracks between Gdynia and Wejherowo.

The crucial demographic fact sitting underneath all of this: Tri-City's de facto population, measured by mobile-phone density rather than registered residence, is roughly 1.1 million in the core and 1.6–1.7 million in OMGGS, the metropolitan association of 59 municipalities. Suburbanization is concentrated in a ring — Banino, Chwaszczyno, Borkowo, Straszyn, Rotmanka, Kowale, Łostowice — and the official register undercounts the real population of those communes by 10–15%.

The trains exist where the population was in 1990. The new population is where there are no trains. This gap is the single most powerful argument that drives every project that follows.

§ 02 — 2026–2028The ribbon-tearing years

The first phase of the decade is dominated not by openings but by closures. SKM ridership in 2026 grows roughly 4% to about 52 million as the last Impulse 2 sets enter service and the new signalling system cuts headway on the spine from 7.5 to under 5 minutes during peaks. But beyond the spine, the network is shrinking before it grows. Line 201 stays closed. Line 202 is a moving construction site.

In autumn 2026, Marshal Mieczysław Struk's office faces its first political test. The KPO-funded Polregio rolling stock order has collapsed because no manufacturer can deliver by the 30 June 2026 deadline. Pomerania's share — about 248 million złoty — must be re-engineered using the EU Modernization Fund and Social Climate Fund. The Marshal negotiates a tripartite arrangement with Newag and Stadler, splitting the order. The decision matters because it locks in dual-mode capability for parts of the network that will never be electrified.

The 2027 opening: Słupsk gets closer

In September 2027, after a delay even longer than the announced one, line 202 between Lębork and Słupsk is delivered with two tracks, ETCS-ready signalling, and new stops at Łebień, Runowo, and Siemianice. Travel time from Gdynia to Słupsk drops by 17 minutes. Polregio's regio service to Słupsk becomes hourly. SKM does not yet extend; the Wejherowo–Lębork section is still single-track.

The 2028 pivot: PKM Południe gets funded

In November 2027, Infra-Centrum Doradztwa delivers PKM Południe documentation three months ahead of schedule. The environmental decision from March 2025 holds. PKM SA submits the construction tender in February 2028.

The PKM Południe tender is the political pivot of the decade. The cost has crept from 2.0 to roughly 2.7 billion złoty between feasibility study and final design, driven by inflation in tunnel boring, by the deeper alignment under Trakt św. Wojciecha, and by the 2,100-space park-and-ride at Kowale. After months of negotiation, the EU Commission approves an out-of-cycle FEnIKS amendment bringing the EU contribution to 1.85 billion złoty — 68% of construction cost.

DEC
2028
After eighteen months of negotiations, the four cities (Gdańsk, Gdynia, Sopot, Pruszcz Gdański) and Pomorskie Voivodeship buy out PKP's 65.98% stake in SKM. Cost: 280 million złoty, financed by the EIB. SKM is consolidated into the same governance structure as PKM SA. The legal merger takes another two years. The operational integration begins immediately.

§ 03 — 2028–2030The skeleton emerges

By the close of 2028, line 201 sections A and B are formally completed: 27 km between Kościerzyna and Somonino, plus the 8 km LK214 spur to Kartuzy, plus 25 km Somonino–Gdańsk Osowa, all electrified, all upgraded for 160 km/h passenger and 120 km/h freight. The Kashubian core suddenly has rail again after 25 years.

The opening of section A and B without section C creates an awkward year in 2029 when Kościerzyna trains terminate at Gdańsk Osowa and passengers must change to PKM. Pomorskie's organizational reflex is to lean into the inconvenience: it brands the temporary service as "PKM Kaszuby" and prices it identically to the rest of the metropolitan ticket. Ridership on the corridor — under 200,000 passengers a year before the closure — climbs to nearly half a million in the first full year.

2029: The first short-turn line is born

The big 2029 milestone is line 202 north of Gdynia. ZUE SA delivers the four-track configuration between Gdynia Chylonia and Wejherowo, a 17-kilometre segment with two "agglomeration tracks" reserved for SKM and two long-distance tracks for Polregio and freight. In December 2029, the timetable change introduces the first short-turn S-line in Tri-City history: trains running every 10 minutes peak between Wejherowo and Gdynia Główna, branded internally as "S2."

Wejherowo's mayor calls it the most important rail event in the town since the 1870s.

2030: The provisional Pruszcz extension

In 2030, the regional government does something that surprises observers: instead of waiting for PKM Południe to open, it launches a provisional SKM extension to Pruszcz Gdański using existing line 9 paths, with two intermediate stops reactivated. The service runs every 20 minutes peak. The technical grade of the service is below SKM standards; the political grade is high. Gdańsk's southern suburbs finally have a city train.

§ 04 — 2030–2033The ring closes, the metropolis becomes legible

The third phase of the decade is when the network stops being a collection of corridors and starts being a system. Three things converge.

The first is line 201 section C, the Gdańsk Osowa–Gdynia Główna twelve kilometres with a third track and full electrification. Without it, agglomeration trains and long-distance trains compete for the same paths between Gdańsk Osowa and Gdynia. With it, a true ring service becomes possible: Gdańsk Główny → Wrzeszcz → PKM → Gdynia Główna in 38 minutes. From the December 2031 timetable change, this loop runs every 20 minutes peak in both directions.

The second is the opening of PKM Południe in May 2032, six months behind schedule. The opening ceremony at Gdańsk Łostowice-Świętokrzyska — a deep tunnel station beneath the southern Gdańsk plateau — draws Marshal Struk, the Prime Minister, the EU Commissioner for Cohesion, and a crowd of roughly 8,000 residents. The new line carries 28,000 passengers daily on its first weekday and reaches 45,000 within six months. The City of Gdańsk's transport model finds that modal share for public transport in southern Gdańsk has jumped from 27% to 41% in two years.

MAY
2032
Travel time from Kowale to Gdańsk Główny: 14 minutes. The Kowale park-and-ride fills to capacity by mid-2033. The City of Gdańsk's transport model, recalibrated in 2034, finds modal share for public transport in southern Gdańsk has jumped from 27% to 41% in two years.

The third is operational and organizational. The PKM-SKM merger, formally completed in mid-2031, allows Pomorskie to consolidate all metropolitan rail services under one operator. Line numbering is rationalized: S1 for the spine, S2 for the Wejherowo short-turn, S3 for the new ring, S4 for PKM Południe. Bilet Metropolitalny is renamed Bilet Pomorski. The metropolitan act of 2030 gives OMGGS formal organizing competence for transport.

§ 05 — 2033–2036The fifth line and the maturing system

The last three years are quieter in construction terms but more interesting politically. The system that exists is not the system that was planned. PKM Południe ridership exceeds all forecasts, but the Kowale terminus is creating intolerable peak congestion. Pomorskie commissions a feasibility study in 2033 for PKM Południe Stage 2: extending from Kowale through Borkowo and Straszyn to a junction at Glincz.

The fifth line — the one stamped on the side of an Impulse 3 in December 2036 — is the Kościerzyna–Wrzeszcz–Gdańsk Główny regional express, integrated into the SKM brand after a year of negotiations between Polregio, the merged PKM-SKM operator, and the Marshal's office. SKM takes over the agglomeration-grade service that runs every 30 minutes peak between Kościerzyna and Wrzeszcz, calling at all stations. The line is branded S5. The branding is contested for months.

Some in Pomorskie wanted to call it RE1 to distinguish it from the agglomeration S-lines, but the political calculation is that Kashubia has been waiting too long for "real SKM" to be denied the letter S.

§ 06 — December 2036The five lines, explained

S1 · Lębork ↔ Pruszcz Gdański

The spine. 87 kilometres, every 7.5 minutes peak between Wejherowo and Pruszcz, every 15 minutes off-peak, hourly through the night. Twenty-eight stations. Carries roughly 55 million passengers annually.

S2 · Wejherowo ↔ Gdynia Główna

The agglomeration short-turn. 25 kilometres on dedicated agglomeration tracks delivered by ZUE in 2029. Every 10 minutes peak. Functions as a frequency overlay on the spine. About 15 million passengers annually.

S3 · Gdańsk Główny ↔ PKM ↔ Gdynia (the ring)

Through-running on PKM line 248 and electrified line 201. Travel time around the loop: 38 minutes. Connects Gdańsk's airport, Olivia Centre, the southern Gdynia office cluster, and three university campuses without ever using the spine. About 12 million passengers annually.

S4 · PKM Południe

Gdańsk Główny ↔ Śródmieście ↔ Łostowice-Świętokrzyska ↔ Kowale. Six stations. Every 7.5 minutes peak. About 18 million passengers annually, with strong reverse-peak demand from Kowale into Gdańsk's southern industrial zones.

S5 · Wrzeszcz ↔ Kościerzyna (Kashubian regional)

60 kilometres. Every 30 minutes peak, hourly off-peak. Eleven stations. Travel time 47 minutes. About 4 million passengers annually — small in volume but transformative for the Kashubian commuter belt.

§ 07 — ConclusionWhat this decade actually teaches

The 2026–2036 decade in Pomerania is not a triumph of vision. It is a triumph of patience — the same plans, restated for a decade, finally meeting the funding cycles and political coalitions that can deliver them. PKM Południe was first sketched in 2010; it opens in 2032. The municipalization of SKM was first proposed in 2014; it completes in 2031. The third track between Osowa and Gdynia was designed in 2018; it carries trains in 2031.

By 2036 Tri-City has five lines because it built, in cumulative effect, a single coherent network out of fragments — SKM's pre-war spine, PKP PLK's modernized long-distance corridors, PKM's regional experiment, and Polregio's reactivated branches. The five lines are less an achievement than a consequence: of EU money that flowed predictably for a decade, of demographic pressure that refused to relent, of regional governance that learned to outwait and out-staff its national counterparts, and of citizens who took 33 train trips a year per capita and made it clear they would take more if more trains were offered.

A spine, a short-turn, a ring, a southern arm, and a Kashubian regional express. Five lines. One metropolis. One ticket.

The reasoning behind the decisions

Five iterations of analysis shaped this narrative. Each iteration interrogated the previous one's assumptions. These are the questions that mattered.

Q.01
Why does PKM Południe come first, before any northern extension?
Three reasons converge. Demographics: the Kowale–Łostowice–Borkowo axis added more residents 2010–2025 than any other Polish suburban corridor outside Warsaw. Existing commitment: by 2026, environmental decisions, design contracts, and city land contributions are already sunk costs. Funding fit: FEnIKS 2021–27 has capital it must deploy by n+3 deadlines, and PKM Południe is the only Pomeranian project of sufficient scale that can absorb 1.85B złoty. The northern Kosakowo alignment, by contrast, has no environmental decision and no design contract in 2026 — it cannot start spending fast enough.
Path-dependence
Q.02
Why does the SKM municipalization happen in 2028, not earlier or later?
Earlier was politically impossible: PKP S.A. valued its 65.98% stake at numbers Pomorskie wouldn't pay. Later was financially impossible: the Pakt Kolejowy commitments require unified governance to coordinate 12+ contracts running concurrently 2028–2032. The 2028 window is when EIB rates were still favorable and when Marshal Struk's negotiating leverage peaked — Pomorskie was simultaneously the largest customer of PKP infrastructure and the largest source of regional rolling stock orders.
Window of opportunity
Q.03
Why is the fifth line Kashubian, not northern (Kosakowo)?
This is the closest call in the narrative. Both could plausibly have been S5. The Kashubian alignment wins because line 201 modernization (already funded) does most of the construction work for free — S5 only needs operator integration, brand alignment, and frequency commitment, not new track. Kosakowo requires fresh contracts, EIA decisions, and roughly 850M złoty in capex that competes directly with Stage 2 of PKM Południe. By the time Stage 2 contract signs in 2034, the Kosakowo case is structurally weaker. Kashubia gets S5 because Kashubia is cheaper to serve.
Marginal cost reasoning
Q.04
What could realistically derail this sequence?
Three credible failure modes. (1) A change of government in 2027 that withdraws KPO/FEnIKS amendments — this is the highest-probability disruption, but Polish rail funding has proven politically robust because EU money is involved. (2) Marshal Struk losing the 2024 election — a credible alternative incumbent does not exist; institutional knowledge would be lost. (3) Construction-cost inflation above 8% annually, which would force PKM Południe Stage 2 to be cancelled or descoped. The most likely real-world divergence is a 12–18 month slip on every milestone, not a structural failure.
Risk analysis
Q.05
Why is "trips per resident" the metric that actually matters?
Total passenger numbers can grow by simply moving the same commuters around more. Modal share can grow by suppressing car usage rather than by genuine network attraction. Trips per resident captures whether daily life has changed. Vienna's 5.6 trips/resident/year and Zürich's 8.1 are the gold standard for European agglomerations. Pomorskie's 33.24 in 2025 already includes long-distance and seasonal trips, but the metropolitan-only figure was around 18. The 2036 target of 48 means a typical Tri-City resident takes a train almost weekly. That is the genuine signal of network success.
Metric design
Q.06
Why does brand consolidation (S1–S5) matter so much?
Customers experience networks, not companies. London ran Overground this way since 2007. Berlin's S-Bahn for decades. The "magic trick" — a Polregio-operated service marketed as SKM — separates corporate boundaries (which are politically rigid) from the customer promise (which can change overnight). One ticket, one app, one badge on the side of the train. Without this consolidation, a passenger faces three operators, three websites, three apps, and three brand identities — which empirically reduces ridership by 8–15% in European studies, regardless of physical network quality.
Brand vs operator

Five durable lessons

For someone learning how rail networks actually develop — not how they appear in glossy strategy documents — Tri-City's decade offers a remarkably clean case study.

01FUNDING
Funding cycles, not engineering, set the timing.

Every major Pomeranian rail project in this decade is shaped by the EU's Multiannual Financial Framework, which runs in seven-year cycles (2014–2020, 2021–2027, 2028–2034). Projects rush to be "n+3" eligible, meaning they must spend their allocation within three years of the cycle ending.

Line 202's slipped 2027 completion costs Pomorskie nothing engineering-wise but jeopardizes 280 million złoty of FEnIKS reimbursement. The PKM Południe construction tender is timed in 2028 not because the design is finished — it could have been ready earlier — but because the FEnIKS amendment cycle aligns.

Never look at a rail project schedule without first asking which EU programme is paying for which segment, and what the n+3 deadline is.
02STRUCTURE
The infrastructure manager and the operator are different organisms with different incentives.

PKP PLK builds the tracks but does not run trains. SKM and Polregio run trains but do not own the rails they run on. PKM SA, uniquely, does both — which is why it can deliver projects faster and integrate operations more tightly than the national norm.

It is also why PKM is more politically vulnerable: a single regional government takes both blame and credit. The 2030 metropolitan act and the 2031 PKM-SKM merger try to push the whole Tri-City network toward the PKM model.

Whether vertical integration improves outcomes by 2040 is one of the open questions of the decade. The bet is that it does.
03DEMOGRAPHICS
Demographics outrun timetables.

The most important data underlying every project is not in any rail document. It is in the Pomorskie Biuro Planowania Regionalnego mobile-phone study showing that 200,000 more people live in the metropolis than the GUS register records, almost all in suburban communes.

The PKM Południe alignment chooses Kowale over Pruszcz Gdański because that is where the people are, not where the historical rail map suggested they would be. Suburbanization is the engine; rail infrastructure is the chassis trying to catch up.

When a network forgets this — as Polish rail did in the 1990s — it loses two decades of ridership.
04METRICS
Success metrics are political before they are technical.

Tri-City's two tracked metrics in 2036 — passengers per year (95 million network-wide) and modal share for public transport (35% metropolitan-wide) — are presented to voters as proof of competence.

But the metric Marshal Struk's office watches in private is trips per resident per year, where Pomorskie holds and extends a national lead. Trips-per-resident captures whether the network is genuinely changing daily life, or merely moving the same commuters around. By 2036 it stands at 48, up from 33.24 in 2024.

The metric you can publish is the one that survives politics. The metric you trust is the one that captures behaviour change.
05COOPERATION
Cooperation pays a premium that no single player can capture.

The Bilet Metropolitalny in 2008. The FALA payment system in 2024. The joint PSC contract in 2033. The four-city buyout of PKP's stake in 2028. Each is a coordination victory whose return shows up in ridership two to five years later, never on the books of the agency that paid for it.

This is why metropolitan transport governance is hard everywhere, and why Pomorskie's eventual win is partly a function of having the same Marshal in office continuously since 2010, building relationships across changes of national government.

Institutional persistence beats institutional brilliance. The single most important variable is who is still in the room after the third election.

Glossary of acronyms

SKM
Szybka Kolej Miejska — Tri-City's commuter rail operator since 1951; majority-owned by PKP until 2028, then by Pomorskie + four cities.
PKM
Pomorska Kolej Metropolitalna — regional rail company that opened the Wrzeszcz–Airport–Osowa line in September 2015. Both infrastructure manager and (after 2031) operator.
PKP PLK
PKP Polskie Linie Kolejowe — Poland's national rail infrastructure manager. Owns most of the track in the country.
FEnIKS
Fundusze Europejskie na Infrastrukturę, Klimat, Środowisko — Poland's main EU cohesion programme for transport infrastructure 2021–2027.
KPO
Krajowy Plan Odbudowy — Poland's National Recovery and Resilience Plan, the EU's post-COVID Recovery and Resilience Facility allocation.
KPK 2030
Krajowy Program Kolejowy — National Rail Programme through 2030. Domestic capital programme for rail infrastructure.
OMGGS
Obszar Metropolitalny Gdańsk-Gdynia-Sopot — voluntary association of 59 municipalities; gains formal organising competence after 2030 metropolitan act.
CPK
Centralny Port Komunikacyjny — Poland's central transport hub project, including the Y-line high-speed rail Warsaw-Łódź-Wrocław-Poznań.
PSC
Public Service Contract — the legal vehicle by which a regional authority orders and pays an operator to run trains on its territory.
ETCS
European Train Control System — the EU-mandated signalling standard. Level 2 enables 200 km/h operation.
EIB
European Investment Bank — provides long-tenor public-sector loans; financed the SKM municipalization buyout in 2028.
n+3
EU funding rule: money allocated in year n must be spent by year n+3, or it expires. Drives most rail project timing.
